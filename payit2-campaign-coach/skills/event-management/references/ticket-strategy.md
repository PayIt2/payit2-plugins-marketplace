## Early Bird Pricing Formulas

Early bird pricing serves two purposes: generate early revenue to fund event costs, and create social proof that drives regular-price sales.

### Formula
```
Early Bird Price = Regular Price x (1 - Discount Rate)
```

### Discount Rate: 25-30% off regular price

### Examples at Common Price Points

| Regular Price | Early Bird (25% off) | Early Bird (30% off) | Savings Message |
|--------------|---------------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| $25 | $18.75 → **$19** | $17.50 → **$17** | "Save $6-8" |
| $50 | $37.50 → **$37** | $35.00 → **$35** | "Save $13-15" |
| $100 | $75.00 → **$75** | $70.00 → **$69** | "Save $25-31" |
| $200 | $150.00 → **$149** | $140.00 → **$139** | "Save $51-61" |

**Note**: Always round to charm prices ($19, $37, $69, $139) — see Price Psychology below.

### Early Bird Parameters
- **Allocation**: Cap at 30% of total capacity. Scarcity drives action.
- **Window**: 7-14 days. Short enough to create urgency, long enough to spread the word.
- **Messaging**: Always show the regular price crossed out next to the early bird price.

## Group Discount Structures

### Standard Group Tiers
| Group Size | Discount | Per-Ticket Price ($50 base) | Total | You Save |
|-----------|---------|---------------------------|-------|----------|
| 1-4 | 0% | $50 | $50-200 | — |
| 5-9 | 10% | $45 | $225-405 | $25-45 |
| 10-19 | 15% | $42.50 → $42 | $420-798 | $80-152 |
| 20+ | 20% | $40 | $800+ | $200+ |

### Break-Even Analysis

Group discounts are profitable when the incremental attendees wouldn't have come at full price.

**Example at $50/ticket, 200 capacity:**
| Scenario | Tickets Sold | Revenue | vs. No Discount |
|----------|-------------|---------|-----------------|
| No group discount | 150 | $7,500 | — |
| With group discount (30 group, 130 individual) | 160 | $7,750 | +$250 |
| With group discount (50 group, 120 individual) | 170 | $8,050 | +$550 |

**Key insight**: Group discounts almost always increase total revenue because groups bring people who wouldn't have bought individually.

### When to Offer Group Discounts
- Corporate events and conferences (teams attend together)
- Sports events (teams, clubs, families)
- Community gatherings (friend groups, church groups)
- Any event where "bringing friends" improves the experience

## BOGO & Promotional Offers

**Key stat**: BOGO offers sell 2.2x more tickets than standard pricing.

### BOGO Templates

**Buy One Get One Free**
- Best for: Events prioritizing attendance over revenue per ticket
- Effective cost: 50% off per ticket
- Messaging: "Bring a friend FREE — because everything's better together"

**Buy One Get One 50% Off**
- Best for: Balanced approach — drives pairs without halving revenue
- Effective cost: 25% off per ticket
- Messaging: "Grab a ticket, get one for a friend at half price"

**Buy 2 Get 1 Free**
- Best for: Driving groups of 3+
- Effective cost: 33% off per ticket
- Messaging: "Squad discount: Buy 2 tickets, third one's on us"

### Promotional Offer Ideas
- **Flash sale**: 24-48 hour window, 20% off, announced on social only
- **Referral discount**: Share your unique link, get $X off for every ticket sold through it
- **Alumni/returning attendee**: 15% loyalty discount for past event attendees
- **Social share unlock**: Post about the event, screenshot it, get a discount code

## Price Psychology Tips

### Charm Pricing
End prices in 7 or 9. $49 feels significantly cheaper than $50 even though the difference is trivial. Use $19, $37, $49, $69, $99, $149, $199.

### Anchor Pricing
Always show your highest tier first (or prominently). A $200 VIP ticket makes the $75 regular ticket feel like a bargain. On the event page, list tiers from most expensive to least.

### Bundle Value
Instead of "$75 for entry," write "$75 — includes entry, lunch, materials, and networking reception ($120 value)." Perceived value should always exceed the price.

### The Decoy Effect
If you want most people to buy the middle tier, create a top tier that's only slightly more expensive than the middle but dramatically more than the base. The middle becomes the "smart choice."

Example:
- General: $49
- VIP: $89 (most will buy this)
- Ultra VIP: $99 (exists to make VIP look smart)

### Scarcity Messaging
- "Only 12 VIP tickets remaining"
- "Early bird ends Friday at midnight"
- "Last year sold out — don't wait"
- Show a progress bar of tickets sold

## Capacity Management

### When to Close Tiers
- **Early bird**: Close at 30% capacity OR end of time window, whichever comes first
- **Regular**: Close when 90% of capacity is sold; switch remaining to "last chance" pricing
- **Any tier**: Close immediately if event logistics can't support more (fire code, catering limits, venue constraints)

### Waitlist Strategy
When a tier sells out:

1. **Enable waitlist immediately** — capture the demand signal
2. **Communicate clearly**: "This tier is sold out. Join the waitlist and we'll notify you if spots open."
3. **Offer alternatives**: "VIP is sold out, but Regular tickets are still available"
4. **Use waitlist data**: If 50+ people are waitlisted, consider adding capacity or scheduling a second event
5. **Release waitlist tickets in order**: First come, first served. Give 24 hours to purchase before moving to the next person.

### Oversell Buffer
For free or low-cost events with historically high no-show rates (20-30%), consider overselling by 10-15%. Track no-show data across events to calibrate.
